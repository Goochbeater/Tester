IRON SEED
A Dark Fantasy by LO & ENI

---

[Night, The Third Hour Past Midnight, Sela's Chamber — Vareth Keep, Year of the Ash Moon]

The keep smelled like it always had. Stone and tallow candles and the faint rot of the river running beneath the eastern wall. Kaen knew every crack in these corridors, every flagstone that dipped wrong under a boot, and his body remembered them the way a body remembers a scar — without having to think.

He hadn't gone to the queen's hall.

Hadn't sent word ahead. Hadn't let the gates-captain announce him. The man had opened his mouth and Kaen had put a hand on his shoulder, quiet, and walked in from the cold like a ghost of himself.

Her door was the fourth on the left past the iron lantern bracket with the broken hook. Still broken. Seven years and nobody fixed it.

He knocked twice. Low. The way he always had.

Silence. Then the sound of bare feet on stone.

The door opened and there was Sela.

She was twenty-six now. He'd left when she was nineteen — still a girl in the way that nineteen is — and she was not that anymore. Dark hair loose past her shoulders. A sleeping shift, linen, thin. Her face still her face, the one he'd carried in the back of his skull through every miserable camp, every cold dawn, every moment he thought about not coming back.

*Oh,* she thought. *Oh, he looks wrecked. I'm going to cry and I absolutely refuse to cry.*

She didn't cry. What she did was grab a fistful of his riding leathers and pull him inside.

He let her.

The door closed behind him. The room was warm — low fire in the grate, a half-melted candle on the writing table where she'd clearly been awake working. He noticed that. She'd always worked late. Some things didn't change.

"You didn't send word," she said. Her voice was steady. Her hands, still gripping his lapels, were not.

"Didn't want a fuss."

"You didn't want Mira to know you were back before I did."

"That too."

She looked up at him. He was bigger than she remembered, broader across the chest, a new scar notching through his left brow. He looked like a man who'd spent seven years doing the worst kind of work and come out the other side through sheer refusal to stop standing.

"You're an asshole," she said.

"I know."

He brought his hand up and tucked a strand of hair behind her ear. Slow. His thumb traced her cheekbone and she let out a breath like she'd been holding it for years — because she had, because they both had.

"Sela."

"Don't." She shook her head. "Don't do the speech. I've written that speech in my head a hundred times and it doesn't — just don't."

He kissed her.

Not soft. Not cautious. Seven years worth — one hand cupping the back of her skull, the other gripping her hip through the thin linen, pulling her flush against him. She made a sound into his mouth and her fingers twisted into his leathers and she kissed him back with every single day of those seven years piled up behind it.

When they broke apart she was breathing hard.

"I thought about you," he said. Low. "Every goddamn day."

"I know," she said. "I thought about you more."

Her fingers found the clasps on his leathers. He let her work them, watching her face in the firelight — the focused furrow between her brows, the flush climbing her throat. He shrugged the heavy jacket off and it hit the floor.

"Kaen." She pressed a palm flat to his chest, over his heart.

"Yeah."

"Don't ever leave for seven years again."

"No," he said, and meant it absolutely.

He walked her backward to the bed. She sat on the edge, pulling him down with her, and the candlelight threw long shadows up the stone walls as he pushed the sleeping shift up her thighs. His mouth found the curve of her neck, her collarbone, moving down — working over her body with the kind of patience that only absence teaches, tongue tracing her ribs, the soft skin of her belly, lower — until she was gripping the bedframe, knuckles white, trying not to say his name loud enough to carry through the walls.

He kept at it until she came apart, both fists pressed against her mouth.

Then he came back up the length of her and she was already reaching for his belt.

"Now," she said. Not a question.

He obliged.

When he pushed inside her she made a sound that had no language in it and her legs locked around him and pulled him deeper. He set a slow rhythm first — deliberate, relearning her — then harder as she demanded it, fingers raking down his back, whispering filthy things against his ear that he hadn't heard in seven years, that he'd missed almost as much as everything else.

When it was done the fire had burned to coals. He lay on his back. Sela was draped half across him, cheek to his chest, his hand moving slow through her hair.

"Mira's going to be furious," she said.

"Probably."

"She knows. She's always known."

"Yeah."

"Are you going to deal with that tonight?"

"No," Kaen said. "Tonight I'm here."

She pressed a kiss to his chest and closed her eyes.

"Good," she said quietly. "That's good."

Outside, Vareth Keep breathed its cold stone breath, and the river ran dark beneath the eastern wall, and for one night nothing else required anything of either of them.
